  2. Our definition of AI

    3. A field of study concerned with the development and use of computer systems that have some resemblance to human intelligence, including such operations as natural-language recognition and use, problem solving, selection from alternatives, pattern recognition, generalization based on experience, and analysis of novel situations. (From Academic Press Dictionary of Science Technology)

    Artificial intelligence (AI) is a field that studies intelligent behavior in humans using the tools-theoretical and experimental-of computer science. The field simultaneously addresses one of the most profound scientific problems-the nature of intelligence-and engages in pragmatically useful undertakings: developing intelligent systems. The concepts, techniques, and technology of AI offer us a number of ways to discover what intelligence is-what one must know to be smart at a particular task-and a variety of computational techniques for embedding that intelligence in a program. (From (Grosz and Davis, 1994) A Report to ARPA on Twenty-First Century Intelligent Systems, Barbara Grosz and Randall Davis, editors. AI Magazine 15(3): Fall 1994, 10-20.)

    AI can be viewed from many different perspectives:
    • From the perspective of intelligence
      artificial intelligence is making machines "intelligent" -- acting as we would expect people to act.

      • The inability to distinguish computer responses from human responses is called the Turing test.

      • Intelligence requires knowledge

      • Expert problem solving - restricting domain to allow including significant relevant knowledge

    • From a research perspective
      "artificial intelligence is the study of how to make computers do things which, at the moment, people do better" [Rich and Knight, 1991, p.3].

    • From a programming perspective, AI includes the study of symbolic programming, problem solving, and search.

      • Typically AI programs focus on symbols rather than numeric processing.

      • Problem solving - achieve goals.

      • Search - seldom access a solution directly. Search may include a variety of techniques.

  3. Our definition of IA

    4. IA is the discipline or set of disciplines devoted to technologically expand our physical or/and cognitive abilities beyond the range of natural humans. [David Brin, The Postman]

    A way of increasing the capability of a man to approach a complex problem situation, to gain comprehension to suit his particular needs, and to derive solutions to problems. Increased capability in this respect is taken to mean a mixture of the following: more-rapid comprehension, better comprehension, the possibility of gaining a useful degree of comprehension in a situation that previously was too complex, speedier solutions, better solutions, and the possibility of finding solutions to problems that before seemed insoluble. (From Douglas C. Engelbart. Augmenting Human Intellect: A Conceptual Framework. Summary Report AFOSR-3223 under Contract AF 49(638)-1024, SRI Project 3578 for Air Force Office of Scientific Research, Stanford Research Institute, Menlo Park, Ca., October 1962.)

  5. Goals


    6. 1. AI

    Develop intelligent computers that emulate intelligent behavior in humans. Intelligent behavior can be characterized by these abilities to:
    1. Respond to situations very flexibly,
    2. make sense out of ambiguous or contradictory messages,
    3. recognize the relative importance of different elements of a situation,
    4. find similarities between situations despite differences which may separate them, or
    5. to draw distinctions between situations despite similarities which may link them

    The field of artificial intelligence (AI) encompasses a variety of activities serving several interrelated scientific and practical aims:
    • Understanding the psychology and behavior of people, animals, and artificial agents,
    • Making intelligent machines, whether these work the same way people do or not, and
    • Formalizing and mechanizing human knowledge, both common sense and expertise, in all areas of human endeavor.
    • Making working with computers as easy and as helpful as working with skilled, cooperative, and possibly expert people.
    • Solve intellectual problems both theoretical and practical, common and esoteric;
    • Control robot motions through planning, sight, and touch;
    • Interpret human language, both written and spoken; and
    • Learn new skills and knowledge from experience and other data.
    (From (Grosz and Davis, 1994) A Report to ARPA on Twenty-First Century Intelligent Systems, Barbara Grosz and Randall Davis, editors. AI Magazine 15(3): Fall 1994, 10-20.)

    2. IA

    1. Boundless Expansion
      Seeking more intelligence, wisdom, and effectiveness, an unlimited lifespan, and the removal of political, cultural, biological, and psychological limits to self-actualization and self-realization. Perpetually overcoming constraints on our progress and possibilities. Expanding into the universe and advancing without end. Rational use of science and technology to eradicate constraints on lifespan, intelligence, personal vitality, freedom, and experience. Recognition of the absurdity of accepting "natural" limits to our lifespans.
    2. Self-Transformation
      Affirming continual moral, intellectual, and physical self-improvement, through reason and critical thinking, personal responsibility, and experimentation. Seeking biological and neurological augmentation through means such as neurochemical enhancers, computers and electronic networks, General Semantics, fuzzy logic, and other guides to effective thinking, meditation and visualization techniques, accelerated learning strategies, applied cognitive psychology, and soon neural- computer integration. Shrugging off the limits imposed on us by our natural heritage, we apply the evolutionary gift of our rational, empirical intelligence to surpass the confines of our humanity, crossing the threshold into the transhuman and posthuman stages that await us.
    3. Intelligent Technology
      Applying science and technology creatively to transcend "natural" limits imposed by our biological heritage, culture, and environment. Technology is a natural extension and expression of human intellect and will, of creativity, curiosity, and imagination. Ever more flexible, smart, responsive technology. Co-evolution with the products of our minds, integration with them and finally merging with our intelligent technology in a posthuman synthesis, amplifying our abilities and extending our freedom. This technological transformation will be accelerated by genetic engineering, life extending biosciences, intelligence intensifiers, smarter interfaces to swifter computers, neural-computer integration, virtual reality, enormous and interconnected databases, swift electronic communications, artificial intelligence, neuroscience, neural networks, artificial life, off-planet migration, and nanotechnology.

  6. HISTORY OF IA

    7. Utilization of external technological tools played the crucial role on all stages of human evolution. From simple tools (physical extensions) to domesticated animals for watching the environment and transportation, and clay tablets and books (external memory), people have been increasingly dependent on external artifacts for enhancements of their physical functions as well as perception and intellectual abilities.

    In recent history, the tools have become more complex and autonomous, performing increasingly sophisticated and abstract functions, and development of extensions to human intelligence has been transformed into a rapidly growing area of both theoretical research and practical system development.

    Some recent landmarks in this process include:


    1945

    • Vannevar Bush describes the "memex" in Atlantic magazine.

    1958
    • Good (17) includes some conjecture about possibilities of intellectual aid to the human by close cooperation with a computer in a rather general way, and also presents a few interesting thoughts about a network model for structuring the conceptual kernels of information to facilitate a sort of self-organizing retrieval system. (Good, I. J., "How Much Science Can You Have at Your Fingertips?" IBM Journal of Research and Development, Vol. 2, No. 4 (October 1958) )

    1960
    • Licklider publishes article titled "Man-Computer Symbiosis" to refer to the close interaction relationship between the man and the computer in mutually beneficial cooperation. He predicted that "in not too many years, human brains and computing machines will be coupled together very tightly, and that the resulting partnership will think as no human being has ever thought and process data in a way not approached by the information-handling machines we know today". He proposes human-computer interactions to expand thought and data processing at the Advanced Research Project Agency (ARPA) of the U.S. Department of Defense (which eventually develops word processing, personal computing, desktop publishing, and spreadsheets, and establishes ARPAnet, the beginning of the Internet)
    • Ulam specifically recommendeds close man-computer interaction in a chapter entitled, "synergesis," where he points out in considerable detail the types of mathematical work which could be aided (Ulam, S. M., A Collection of Mathematical Problems, p. 135 (Interscience Publishers, Inc.,New York, N.Y., 1960)).
    • Fein makes a comprehensive projection of the growth and dynamic inter-relatedness of "computer-related sciences," includes specific mention of the enhancement of human intellect by cooperative activity of men, mechanisms, and automata. He coins the term "synnoetics" as applicable generally to the cooperative interaction of people, mechanisms, plant or animal organisms, and automata into a system whose mental power is greater than that of its components, and presents a good picture of the integrated way in which many separate disciplines should be developed and taught in the future to do justice to their mutual roles in the important metadiscipline defined as "synnoetics." (Fein, Louis, "The Computer-Related Science (Synnoetics) at a University in the Year 1975," unpublished paper (December 1960)).

    1961
    • Ramo gives a number of talks and writes some articles dealing with the future possibilities of computers for "extending man's intellect". His projections are slanted more toward larger bodies of humans interacting with computers, in less of an intimate personal sense. (Ramo, Simon, "The Scientific Extension of the Human Intellect," Computers and Automation (February 1961)).

    1963
    • Douglas C. Engelbart establishes the Augmentation Research Center at the Stanford Research Institute and publishes "A Conceptual Framework for the Augmentation of Man's Intellect".

    1970
    • Alan Kay publishes The Art of Human-Computer Interaction.

    1977
    • Ted Nelson publishes The Home Computer Revolution .

    1985
    • Howard Rheingold publishes Tools for Thought (Simon and Schuster).

    In the last 10 years, there has been an explosion of both practical and theoretical work on the subject. This has also been a decade when practical usage of IA technology spread to the homes of practically all educated people, and millions of people became interested in the cutting edge and promises of advanced intelligence augmentation tools. This includes fast computers with graphical interfaces, microprocessors in many household appliances, massive computerization of workplaces, global communication networks including Internet, universal acceptance of email, pagers, cellular phones, teleconferencing and other tools of electronic communications, digital signal processing, personal assistants, explosive growth of the Web, proliferation of cryptography, tools for manipulating graphics, sounds and video streams, electronics security systems, etc.

    More advanced areas of research and development include interactive 3-D modeling, extensive studies in ergonomics, speech recognition, Virtual and Enhanced Reality, automated translation, Software agents technologies, Neural Networks, Alife and genetic programming, industrial robotics, digital cash, telepresence, nanotechnology, etc.

  11. Power

    12. The computer savants correctly notice that computerization alters relationships of social power and control; however, the most obvious beneficiaries of this change are large transnational business corporations. While their "global reach" does not arise solely from the application of information technologies, such organizations are uniquely situated to exploit the new electronic possibilities for greater efficiency, productivity, command, and control. Other notable beneficiaries will be public bureaucracies, intelligence agencies, and ever-expanding military organizations.

    Ordinary people are, of course, strongly affected by these organizations and by the rapid spread of new electronic systems in banking, insurance, taxation, work, home entertainment, and the like. They are counted on to be eventual eager buyers of hardware, software, and communications services.

    But where is any motion toward increased democratization and social equality, or the dawn of a cultural renaissance? Current empirical studies of computers and social change - such as those described in Computers and Politics by James Danzig - suggest an increase in power by those who already have a great deal of power, an enhanced centralization of control by those already in control, and an augmentation of wealth by the already wealthy. If there is to be a computer revolution, it will most likely have a distinctly conservative character.

    With Intelligence Augmentation, the risk of an even more polarized society, a world dominated by the few privileged who had been augmented and therefore enjoy of much more powerful tools dramatically increases. Will IA be a privilege or a right? It is a challenge -our challenge- to ensure a democratic augmentation of society. Who will decide whom to augment? How? When? Where?
